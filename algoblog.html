Your Kid Doesn't Need Better Foods.
They Need a Better Path.

March 2026 • 6 min read

Every time Sensory Galaxy suggests a new food, it's not guessing. It's running a calculation built around your child—their actual foods, their real progress, where they are right now. Here's how it works.

Start With What They Already Eat

When you first set up a profile, you tell us the foods your child already eats reliably. These become the foundation. We call them safe foods. But not all safe foods are equal.

A food your child has eaten every day for three years carries more weight than one they've only had a few times. So right from the start, we're building a picture that reflects reality, not just a checklist.

That picture becomes their sensory baseline—a profile of the textures, flavors, temperatures, and other properties that define their current comfort zone. Think of it as a sensory fingerprint. Unique to them. And always changing.

Bridge Foods, Not Leaps

If your kid only eats chicken nuggets and rice, the problem probably isn't that they need to try more things. The problem is that the jump from nuggets to broccoli is too big. There's too much that's unfamiliar all at once. The sensory distance is too far.

Bridge foods close that gap. They're foods that share one or two sensory properties with what your child already eats—maybe the same texture, maybe similar temperature—but introduce something new in a way that feels achievable.

But finding them means understanding where your child actually is sensorily. Not where you wish they were. Not where other kids are. Where they are. Today.

The Five Steps That Matter

The Sequential Oral Sensory (SOS) approach, developed by Dr. Kay Toomey, teaches us that eating isn't the only milestone. A child progresses through steps. Each one is real progress.

Looking — Being near the food without distress
Touching — Making physical contact
Smelling — Engaging with the aroma
Tasting — Putting it in the mouth
Eating — Swallowing and incorporating it

Sensory Galaxy tracks all of them. When you log a session, you're not just saying "they tried it." You're saying exactly what happened. That granularity matters. A food your child tasted carries far more weight than one they looked at.

Recency. It Changes Everything.

Your child isn't the same eater they were six months ago. The algorithm knows this. It gives recent experiences more weight than old ones. A food they tried last week shapes the baseline more than a food they ate last fall.

This means the recommendations shift as your kid shifts. If they're going through a phase of exploring crunchy, salty foods this month, the algorithm notices. If that phase changes next month, it adapts. It's always tracking the present, not locked into the past.

Measuring Sensory Distance

Once we have a baseline, the algorithm measures how far away each food in our database is from that baseline. How similar is it? How different?

A short distance means the food shares most of the sensory properties your child already accepts—same texture, similar flavor profile, comparable temperature. A long distance means it's a bigger stretch.

But we don't weight all sensory properties equally. Texture and temperature tend to be higher barriers for selective eaters than, say, color. The algorithm reflects this reality.

Three Recommendations, Three Jobs

Every day, Sensory Galaxy suggests three foods. They look similar on screen, but each one is doing something different. They're drawn from different zones of sensory distance.

Safe Pick

Closest to what they know. High likelihood of acceptance. Reinforcement and confidence.

Stretch Pick

Where growth happens. Close enough to feel possible. Different enough to introduce something new.

Variety Pick

Bridging a food group they haven't explored. Satisfying nutrition without a sensory leap.

But here's the key: the algorithm respects momentum. If your child is gravitating toward sweet foods, the stretch pick won't suddenly land them in front of bitter vegetables. It extends the current direction by one degree.

Spotting the Momentum

One thing that makes Sensory Galaxy different is that it doesn't just look at what your child has eaten. It looks at where they're going.

After each session, the algorithm analyzes the recent foods your child has engaged with. Is there a pattern? Are they gravitating toward sweet? Crunchy? Warm foods?

When a clear trend emerges—say, most of the foods they've explored recently share a dominant flavor or texture—the engine uses that as a signal. It leans into that direction rather than fighting it. Growth feels natural, not random.

When Things Go Backward

Any parent of a selective eater knows this. A food your child ate happily for two weeks suddenly gets refused. It's normal. The algorithm handles it without drama.

It tracks not just the highest exposure level your child ever achieved, but what happened most recently. Progress is preserved in the long-term record. But today's recommendations are always calibrated to today's child.

So When You See a Suggestion...

It's been filtered through your child's entire logged history. Weighted by recency. Measured against sensory distance. Checked against detected patterns. Validated against nutritional gaps.

It should just feel like the app knows your child. Because in the ways that matter most to feeding therapy, it does.
