About Selective Eating & Sensory Sensitivity

What's the difference between "picky eating" and sensory sensitivity?

Good question. They're totally different.

Picky eating is usually behavioral. Kids might refuse new foods because they're being stubborn, or they want control, or they've learned that complaining gets them what they want.

Sensory sensitivity is neurological. Your child's nervous system literally processes textures, tastes, and smells differently. It's not that they won't try broccoli—it's that broccoli genuinely feels wrong to them. The texture might feel overwhelming, or the smell too strong, or the temperature jarring.

With picky eating, rewards and consequences might help. With sensory sensitivity, pressure actually makes things worse. Your child needs time and safety to explore.

How common is sensory-sensitive eating?

Very common. About 1 in 3 children experience selective eating tied to sensory sensitivity. You're not alone—not even close.

It's especially common in kids on the autism spectrum, kids with ADHD, and kids with anxiety. But you don't have to have a diagnosis for sensory sensitivity to be real and significant.

Will my child grow out of this?

Sensory sensitivity doesn't just go away on its own. But here's the good news: it's rewirable.

With the right approach (like the SOS Method), kids learn to tolerate and eventually enjoy new foods. The nervous system can adapt. Research shows that kids using the SOS approach see real progress in weeks to months—not years of stress.

So will they grow out of it? Maybe eventually. But you don't have to wait and hope. You can actively help them expand their world.

Why does forcing or bribing my child make it worse?

When a child's nervous system says "this feels wrong," telling them "just try it" or "I'll give you a reward" doesn't change the nervous system. It just adds stress and fear.

Forcing can actually strengthen the aversion. The child learns: "This food makes my parent angry," or "My body is wrong," or "I can't trust my own instincts." That makes everything harder.

That's why the SOS approach works differently. Instead of pressure, you give your child time and safety to explore at their own pace. The nervous system relaxes. Learning happens naturally.

About the SOS Approach

What are the 5 sensory phases?

The SOS (Sequential Oral Sensory) approach breaks down food exploration into 5 phases, each giving the nervous system time to process:

  • LOOK: Just seeing the food. No pressure. Your child gets used to how it looks.
  • TOUCH: Exploring the texture with hands. Squeezing, poking, playing. Building comfort.
  • SMELL: Bringing the food close, smelling it. Letting the nervous system know it's safe.
  • LICK: A tiny taste on the tongue. Your child controls this completely. They can spit it out.
  • TASTE: Actually eating. By now, the nervous system has had time to adjust. Eating feels less shocking.

Each phase typically takes 3-10 days. No rushing. The key is: your child moves at their own pace.

Do I have to follow the phases in order?

Mostly yes, but not rigidly. Here's how to think about it:

The phases go LOOK → TOUCH → SMELL → LICK → TASTE in order because that's the easiest path for the nervous system. It's gradual. Safe.

But if your child jumps ahead (like, they see broccoli and immediately lick it), that's fine. Just celebrate the progress, don't force them back.

The phases are a guide, not a prison. The goal is progress, not perfection.

What if my child refuses to even look at a food?

That's okay. That actually tells you something important: they're not ready yet. Or you picked a food that's too big a jump.

Here's what to do:

  • Don't force it. Move on to something else.
  • Try a different food with more sensory overlap (like, if they like soft mashed potatoes, try sweet potato instead of broccoli)
  • Or try the same food again in a few weeks. Sometimes kids just need time.
  • That's where bridge foods come in—finding foods that are a smaller step from what they already like
Is this backed by science?

Yes. The SOS approach has 30+ years of research behind it. It was developed by occupational and speech therapists who specialize in feeding disorders.

Studies show that kids using the SOS approach consistently see improvements in food acceptance, quality of life, and family mealtimes. It's not a miracle cure, but it's proven to work.

What are "bridge foods"?

Bridge foods are foods that share sensory qualities with foods your child already likes, but introduce something slightly new.

Example: If your child likes crunchy, salty crackers, a bridge food might be crunchy, salty pretzels (similar texture, new taste). Then maybe crunchy, salty breadsticks. Then gradually exploring other crunchy foods.

Bridge foods are the secret to expansion. They're not random. The app uses AI to suggest foods that match your child's sensory profile, so they're actually ready for the next step.

How does the AI recommendation system work?

After your child tries 3-5 foods, our AI (trained on the SOS method) analyzes their sensory preferences and suggests bridge foods they're likely to accept.

How it works:

  • It looks at textures, flavors, temperatures your child gravitates toward
  • It finds foods in our database that have similar qualities but introduce one new element
  • It ranks suggestions by risk level (low-risk = very similar; medium = one difference; high = bigger jump)
  • It avoids nonsensical combos (like "salty liquid meals") with validation rules

It's not magic. It's pattern matching based on the SOS method.

What are the 8 planets?

The 8 planets are your child's journey through the SOS approach. Each planet has 5 phases (LOOK → TOUCH → SMELL → LICK → TASTE), and your child progresses as they complete phases with different foods.

The planets:

  • Planet 1: Base Camp (safe, familiar foods)
  • Planet 2: Sensory Grove (exploring textures)
  • Planet 3: Texture Trails (expanding within comfort zone)
  • Planet 4: Aroma Airship (using smell to explore)
  • Planet 5: Flavor Mountains (introducing new flavors)
  • Planet 6: Taste Ocean (full tasting phases)
  • Planet 7: Taste Jungle (exploring diversity)
  • Planet 8: Galaxy's Heart (mastery and confidence)

It's gamification, but grounded in real progress. Your child can see how far they've come.
