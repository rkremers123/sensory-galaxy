POST 1.2: Education Post — Sensory Fingerprint

What is a sensory fingerprint? Your child doesn't refuse foods randomly. Their nervous system is reading every food along seven dimensions: • Texture (liquid → hard) • Sweetness • Saltiness • Bitterness • Sourness • Savouriness • Temperature A child comfortable with soft, sweet, warm foods will struggle with hard, bitter, cold ones. Not stubbornness. Sensory mismatch. When you log foods in Sensory Galaxy, the app builds their unique sensory fingerprint. Every recommendation bridges one dimension at a time. Not all seven at once. That's what makes it work. Does this match what you've observed with your own picky eater?

POST 2.1: Framework Post — SOS Approach

A sniff is not a failure. In the Sequential Oral Sensory (SOS) approach to feeding therapy, eating a food is just the final step. Not the only milestone. Here's the progression: 👀 Looked (0.3x weight) — Just seeing the food without distress 👆 Touched (0.5x weight) — Making physical contact 👃 Smelled (0.7x weight) — Engaging the aroma 😋 Tasted (0.9x weight) — In the mouth, even if spit out 🍴 Ate (1.0x weight) — Swallowed and incorporated Every step is real progress. A child who touches a food they've never touched before has overcome something. That should be celebrated and counted, not dismissed as "still not eating it." Sensory Galaxy tracks all five. Because progress isn't binary. How many of these steps has your kid reached with their most-challenged foods?

POST 3.1: Technical Insight — Recency Weighting

Your kid is different this week than last week. Last month, your child ate pasta every single day. Today, they're refusing it. Most apps would anchor their recommendations to "pasta is a safe food" and keep suggesting pasta-adjacent foods. But that misses what's actually happening: your child has changed. Sensory Galaxy weights *recent* food logs much more heavily than old ones. A food your child ate last week carries full weight. A food from three weeks ago? Down to 25%. A month ago? 10%. This means our algorithm is always reflecting *this week's child*, not last month's version. It's a small technical decision with a big human impact: we're not locked into yesterday's preferences. Has your kid's preferences shifted dramatically over the past month?

POST 4.1: Deep Dive — Sensory Distance

Why texture matters more than color. When we calculate the sensory distance between two foods, not all dimensions are equal. A child won't refuse broccoli because it's green. They'll refuse it because it's crunchy, or bitter, or both. Texture and temperature are the *real* barriers for selective eaters. In our distance calculation, texture mismatches count for 2.0x more than color mismatches. Why? Because that's what the data shows. That's what SOS-trained therapists see in practice. Every weight in the algorithm is intentional. Built on real observation, not guessing. That's what makes a recommendation actually land. What sensory property does your child struggle with most—texture, temperature, or taste?

POST 4.2: Product Feature — Three Recommendations

Three recommendations. Three jobs. Every day, Sensory Galaxy suggests three foods. But they're not three random options. **Safe Pick** — Something very close to what your kid already loves. The goal here is *reinforcement*. Another win. Confidence building. **Stretch Pick** — A genuine challenge, but built on familiar ground. This is where real growth happens. Texture changes, flavor shifts, but nothing is a complete shock. **Variety Pick** — Addressing nutritional gaps. Maybe your kid hasn't eaten any vegetables that are cooked. This recommendation finds the cooked vegetable closest to their current baseline. Each pick is doing something different. Each one has a fallback if nothing in that category is suitable. That structure is why recommendations actually work instead of feeling random. Which of these three would you expect your kid to respond to best?
