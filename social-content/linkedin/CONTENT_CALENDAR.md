```
What is a sensory fingerprint?

Your child doesn't refuse foods randomly. Their nervous system is reading 
every food along seven dimensions:

• Texture (liquid → hard)
• Sweetness
• Saltiness
• Bitterness
• Sourness
• Savouriness

When you log foods in Sensory Galaxy, the app builds their unique sensory
```

## **WEEK 2: Clinical Credibility**

### **POST 2.1 — MONDAY (Slide 4)**
**Headline:** Framework Post

**Copy:**
```
A sniff is not a failure.

In the Sequential Oral Sensory (SOS) approach to feeding therapy, eating 
a food is just the final step. Not the only milestone.

Here's the progression:

👀 **Looked (0.3x weight)** — Just seeing the food without distress
👆 **Touched (0.5x weight)** — Making physical contact
👃 **Smelled (0.7x weight)** — Engaging the aroma
😋 **Tasted (0.9x weight)** — In the mouth, even if spit out
🍴 **Ate (1.0x weight)** — Swallowed and incorporated

Every step is real progress. A child who touches a food they've never touched 
before has overcome something. That should be celebrated and counted, not 
dismissed as "still not eating it."

Sensory Galaxy tracks all five. Because progress isn't binary.
```

## **WEEK 3: The Intelligence Layer**

### **POST 3.1 — MONDAY (Slide 5)**
**Headline:** Technical Insight

**Copy:**
```
Your kid is different this week than last week.

Last month, your child ate pasta every single day. Today, they're refusing it.

Most apps would anchor their recommendations to "pasta is a safe food" and 
keep suggesting pasta-adjacent foods.

But that misses what's actually happening: your child has changed.

Sensory Galaxy weights *recent* food logs much more heavily than old ones. 
A food your child ate last week carries full weight. A food from three weeks 
ago? Down to 25%. A month ago? 10%.

This means our algorithm is always reflecting *this week's child*, not 
last month's version.

It's a small technical decision with a big human impact: we're not locked 
into yesterday's preferences.

```

## **WEEK 4: How Recommendations Work**

### **POST 4.1 — MONDAY (Slide 7)**
**Headline:** Deep Dive

**Copy:**
```
Why texture matters more than taste color.

When we calculate the sensory distance between two foods, not all dimensions 
are equal.

A child won't refuse broccoli because it's green. They'll refuse it because 
it's crunchy, or bitter, or both. Texture and temperature are the *real* 
barriers for selective eaters.

In our distance calculation, texture mismatches count for 2.0x more than 
color mismatches. Why? Because that's what the data shows. That's what 
SOS-trained therapists see in practice.

Every weight in the algorithm is intentional. Built on real observation, 
not guessing.

That's what makes a recommendation actually land.

[IMAGE: Concentric circle diagram from Slide 7 showing distance calculation 
and mismatch penalties]
```

### **POST 4.2 — THURSDAY (Slide 8)**
**Headline:** Product Feature Post

**Copy:**
```
Three recommendations. Three jobs.

Every day, Sensory Galaxy suggests three foods. But they're not three random 
options.

**Safe Pick** — Something very close to what your kid already loves. The goal 
here is *reinforcement*. Another win. Confidence building.

**Stretch Pick** — A genuine challenge, but built on familiar ground. This is 
where real growth happens. Texture changes, flavor shifts, but nothing is a 
complete shock.

**Variety Pick** — Addressing nutritional gaps. Maybe your kid hasn't eaten 
any vegetables that are cooked. This recommendation finds the cooked vegetable 
closest to their current baseline.

Each pick is doing something different. Each one has a fallback if nothing 
in that category is suitable.

That structure is why recommendations actually work instead of feeling random.

[IMAGE: 3-column table from Slide 8 showing the three pick types and their purposes]
```
